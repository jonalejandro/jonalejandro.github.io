Over the course of my career, I’ve worked across three domains in semiconductors: RF, Analog, and Power.

On paper, they all fall under “analog engineering.” In practice, they shape how you think in completely different ways—about systems, products, and ultimately, value.

This isn’t a technical breakdown. It’s a reflection on what each domain teaches you, and how that perspective evolves as you move closer to system-level impact.

---

## RF: Learning Respect for Physics

RF is where you lose the illusion of control early.

At high frequencies, parasitics dominate, layout becomes inseparable from design, and simulation doesn’t always match reality. Small mistakes don’t stay small—they get amplified. You quickly learn that you’re not dictating behavior; you’re working within constraints that are often unforgiving.

I remember a design early on where a seemingly minor layout tweak shifted performance just enough to fail spec, despite simulations looking clean.

This forces a level of discipline that’s hard to replicate elsewhere. Details matter more than expected, and system-level effects show up earlier than you’d like.

From a product standpoint, RF tends to be performance-driven and tightly coupled to standards and ecosystems. Excellence is visible and measurable, but over time, differentiation can compress as solutions mature and integrate.

What RF leaves you with is a mindset: respect the physics, and assume complexity is always closer than it appears.

---

## Analog: Learning to Navigate Tradeoffs

My time working on analog systems at Texas Instruments shifted my perspective from pure performance to optimization.

Analog design lives in tradeoffs. Noise, bandwidth, power, and accuracy are always in tension. There is no perfect solution—only better compromises depending on the application.

This is where technical thinking starts to expand into system thinking. A small change in one part of the circuit can ripple through the entire signal chain. Understanding the context of how a device is used becomes just as important as how it’s designed.

Analog teaches you to think in gradients instead of absolutes. It builds intuition around how systems behave, not just how circuits function.

More importantly, it starts to connect engineering decisions to real-world outcomes. You begin to see how design choices affect performance, usability, and ultimately customer value.

---

## Power: Where Engineering Meets Reality

Power is where everything becomes tangible.

Unlike RF or traditional analog, power isn’t just about signals—it’s about energy moving through a system. Efficiency, thermal limits, transient response, and reliability all converge in ways that are impossible to ignore.

What stands out immediately is how directly improvements translate into impact. A marginal gain in efficiency can reduce system cost. Better regulation can stabilize performance under load. Higher integration can simplify design and accelerate time to market.

In one project, improving efficiency by what looked like a small percentage on paper translated into meaningful thermal headroom at the system level.

Through work on applications ranging from AI processors to SSDs and connected devices, one thing becomes clear: power is not a supporting function. It is an enabling layer.

It sits everywhere, influences everything, and becomes increasingly difficult to replace once designed in. That combination makes it both technically challenging and strategically important.

---

## A Shift in Perspective

Early in my career, I thought about these domains in terms of difficulty.

RF felt the most complex.  
Analog felt the most nuanced.  
Power felt more application-driven.

Over time, that framing became less useful.

A better question emerged:

Where does your work actually move the system?

That question changes how you evaluate everything—not just technology, but roles, opportunities, and long-term direction.

---

## Where the Leverage Is

Each domain optimizes for something different.

RF pushes performance to the edge of physics.  
Analog refines precision through tradeoffs.  
Power scales systems through efficiency.

But from a system perspective, their roles aren’t equal.

RF often differentiates at the front end.  
Analog connects and conditions signals across the chain.  
Power, however, is present everywhere.

As systems scale—especially in areas like AI infrastructure, high-performance storage, and edge computing—the primary constraints are no longer just compute or signal integrity. They are power density, thermal limits, and efficiency at scale.

In that environment, power doesn’t just support the system. It defines its boundaries.

---

## The Career Insight

One of the most valuable shifts in thinking is moving away from asking:

“What is the most technically interesting?”

to asking:

“Where does this work create the most leverage?”

Leverage shows up where technical decisions have outsized impact—on system performance, cost, scalability, and long-term adoption.

Understanding that changes how you choose problems, teams, and domains.

---

## Final Thought

RF, analog, and power are all foundational disciplines. Each one is deep, challenging, and worth mastering.

But they teach you different ways to think.

RF teaches respect for physics.  
Analog teaches mastery of tradeoffs.  
Power teaches how systems actually scale.

Recognizing those differences—and choosing where to go deeper—is one of the highest-leverage decisions you can make in this industry.

If you're early in your career, optimize for exposure across domains.
